Master's Thesis Project

Project Overview: A Usability and Universal Design Investigation into Carousel Interaction in Mobile Contexts

My master's thesis investigated the impact of carousel elements on user experience in a mobile shopping context. The goal was to determine if carousels, a popular design feature for saving space, truly improve usability and user satisfaction. To explore this, I designed and tested two versions of a mobile shopping app: one with carousels and one without.

Methodology: Design, Prototyping, and User Testing

I created two interactive mobile prototypes in Figma, both simulating a mobile clothing store with identical content and a shared visual identity. The design was tailored to a set of 5 specific shopping-related tasks the participants would be asked to complete. I conducted a within-subjects usability test with 30 participants, who completed these tasks on both prototypes. During the tests, I collected quantitative data on task duration and error count, along with qualitative data from user satisfaction ratings and a follow-up interview with a participant with a physical disability.

View With Carousel Prototype View Without Carousel Prototype

Key Findings & Analysis

The results consistently showed that the non-carousel version of the app was more effective. Participants completed tasks faster, made significantly fewer errors, and reported a higher overall satisfaction with the vertical layout. The carousel version introduced cognitive load and navigational friction, leading to a poorer user experience. This suggests that for goal-oriented tasks like shopping, simpler, more direct layouts are more intuitive and user-friendly. The interview with the disabled participant, who had muscular dystrophy, provided a nuanced perspective, revealing that while he found horizontal swipes physically less demanding, the small navigation controls and auto-rotating nature of the carousels were still a significant barrier to usability and control.
